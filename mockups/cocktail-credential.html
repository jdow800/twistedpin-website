Deliverable #2 — four options

Four lines, one structure.

All four lead with the program, close with the credential. They vary on attitude (neutral → with-attitude), length (tight → longest), and how explicit they are about the bowling-alley contradiction. My recommendation is the third one — reasoning below the grid.

Option A · neutral, refined Length · short-mid
A craft cocktail program.
Designed by the head mixologist from Thomas Keller's Per Se.
Tone Direct, factual · Attitude None

Closest to your example. Polished, no edge. The credential anchor is "Thomas Keller's Per Se" — does the heavy lifting on its own. Best for craft-bar landing page (/craft-bar/) where context is already premium and you don't need to argue.

Option B · positioning-first Length · longest
The bar program is the headline.
Designed by the former head mixologist at Thomas Keller's Per Se — Michelin three-star.
Tone Positioning-forward · Attitude Quiet

Names your thesis explicitly in sentence one — useful when you want the visitor to know bowling isn't the lead. Closer carries the full anchor (Keller + Per Se + Michelin three-star). Best for an "About the bar" section block, not the hero.

Option C · with attitude Recommended
A real cocktail bar.
Built by Per Se's former head mixologist.
Tone Direct + slight edge · Attitude On-brand

Tightest. "A real cocktail bar" carries the soft Outlaw jab — implies what the visitor expects from a bowling venue and refuses it, without naming the contradiction. "Built by" is more concrete than "designed by" — implies the room itself, not just a menu consult. Per Se alone carries the anchor at this length; spelling out Michelin or Keller would over-explain.

Option D · names the contradiction Length · long
Cocktails this serious aren't supposed to live at bowling alleys.
Ours were built by Per Se's former head mixologist.
Tone Most edged · Attitude High

Names the contradiction out loud rather than implying it. Riskier — pushes hardest on the Outlaw register, gets closest to the "Built for adults. Kids will come." spice. Strong for the closing band or a press-style pull-quote; on the homepage it competes with the locked closing line for register, so use carefully.

Recommendation

Option C · "A real cocktail bar."

Honors every voice rule: direct, slightly understated, credential as closer, no cute drink-as-device framing. Holds the Outlaw register without reaching for it — the word "real" does the work that "INSANE" or "EPIC" would in a worse brand. And "Per Se" alone is enough at this length; the audience that matters knows what Per Se means, and the audience that doesn't will trust the confidence of the line.

  • Tightest. Two short sentences. Reads as a single typographic block — works as hero subhead, section headline, or marquee divider without rewriting.
  • "Built by" beats "designed by." Implies the bar itself was built around him, not that he passed through and signed a menu. More credible.
  • "A real cocktail bar." Carries the soft jab without spelling out the contradiction (D does that). Reads as confident rather than defensive.
  • Per Se alone is enough at this length. Adding Michelin three-star or Thomas Keller would over-explain and break the rhythm. Save the full credential for /craft-bar/ body copy where there's room.
